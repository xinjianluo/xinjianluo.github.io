Bio

I am a researcher at Mohamed bin Zayed University of Artificial Intelligence (MBZUAI), working closely with Prof. Ting Yu. I recently completed my Ph.D. at the School of Computing, National University of Singapore (NUS), under the guidance of Prof. Xiaokui Xiao.

In 2019, I received my master's degree in Computer Science from Shanghai Jiao Tong University (SJTU), where I worked with Prof. Xiaofeng Gao on my master's thesis. Additionally, I collaborated with Prof. Xiaochun Yang on distributed metadata management and Prof. Qianni Deng on designing two-stage exams to enhance student performance. Prior to joining SJTU, I earned my bachelor's degree in Software Engineering from Dalian University of Technology in 2016, where I worked with Prof. Bin Liu on computer-assisted surgeries and Prof. Cheng Guo on secret image sharing. These experiences have enabled me to accumulate valuable insights, hone my technical skills, and gain a better understanding of the computer science landscape.

My Research Philosophy

I love creating simple yet powerful attacks on the latest ML applications and systems. Why? First, it's the perfect excuse to dive deep into cutting-edge tech, constantly learning how the newest tools work. Second, it lets me uncover the hidden quirks — both vulnerabilities and strengths — of these systems, which is endlessly thrilling. Improving existing attacks? Not my cup of tea. For me, the joy lies in exploring the unknown, not refining what's already been done. That's why you'll never see me working on the same target application twice — every publication is a fresh adventure with new discoveries.

Defenses? Let's just say they don't spark the same excitement. Empirical defenses are like a cat-and-mouse game: you patch one hole, and another attack pops up. Encryption-based defenses are so focused on security that they forget utility — like entering a car race with a tank instead of a car. Differential Privacy (DP), while promising, trades performance for security, which isn't always appealing.

Here's how I see it: building ML applications is like racing a car through treacherous terrain. Empirical defenses are the pit stop for a tire change when you hit a nail — useful, but reactive. Encryption-based defenses? They turn your car into a tank — safe, but slow. DP defenses are like tempered glass upgrades — better safety, but at the cost of speed. The problem is that everyone wants the fastest car to win. Designing attacks, then, is like spotting vulnerabilities in these racing cars beforehand and warning drivers before disaster strikes. It's proactive, impactful, and, most importantly, exhilarating.
